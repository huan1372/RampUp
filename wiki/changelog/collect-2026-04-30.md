## Key Findings

### EAGLE3 Production Validation: Stable Acceptance at Scale (arXiv 2604.19767)

The most significant finding today is a concrete production validation of EAGLE3 spec decode via vLLM on a real commerce agent workload. Previous KB entries documented EAGLE3's vLLM integration (v0.19.0 support, v0.20.0 CUDA graph capture) and P-EAGLE's superiority over EAGLE3 in synthetic benchmarks — but no prior entry had production acceptance rate data at serving scale.

Key insight from this paper: **acceptance rate stability**. The ~35.5% acceptance rate at gamma=3 held flat from concurrency=1 through concurrency=32, across both deterministic (temp=0) and stochastic (temp=0.5) sampling. This is in contrast to P-EAGLE's diminishing returns at c=64 (documented in the P-EAGLE section). The difference is likely workload-driven: commerce agent outputs are structurally predictable (formatted product/commerce responses), while P-EAGLE's diminishing returns were measured on MT-Bench/HumanEval (higher-entropy).

**Economic implication**: the 50% GPU cost reduction (single H100 + EAGLE3 matches 2×H100 NIM) is the most practically actionable result. EAGLE3 is already integrated in vLLM; this paper quantifies the cost case for enabling it on production SLM workloads.

**Relationship to existing KB content**:
- Complements P-EAGLE section: P-EAGLE beats EAGLE3 in synthetic benchmarks but EAGLE3 shows better concurrency scaling on production SLM workloads
- Partially answers the open question "How does EAGLE-3 compare to vanilla speculative decoding in practice?" — it outperforms NIM (which likely uses optimized non-speculative baseline) by 22–49%
- Adds concrete acceptance rate data (35.5% for gamma=3) to calibrate expectations; previously the KB had no production acceptance numbers

**Open question resolved** (partially): "How does EAGLE-3 compare to vanilla speculative decoding in practice?" → On a fine-tuned 8B commerce SLM deployed via vLLM, EAGLE3 at gamma=3 provides 22–49% throughput gain over NIM with no quality loss.
